Green Chili

“Chili Verde con Cerdo”

Words and recipie Andrew Fullerton

When rain falls on the great Contiental Divide of the Americas the water follows one of two paths. If it falls on the east face of the divide it runs and fills the SOMETHING SOMETHING. On the west face it fills rivers like the Rio Grande, A river that flows through the Southwest and Texas and gives water to some of the best green peppers in the world. These peppers go onto make extrodenary meals such as chile rellenos, salsas, and green chili.

Green chili is something of a panacea for me. Growing up on the outskirts of the American Southwest. Green Chili, either alone or smothering a delcious burrito, has been a staple of the Southwest and the Rockies for centuries. It has warmed me up on cold winter days, cured me of disabling mornings after a long night out. It's a salty, rich and sometimes a little sweet indulgence that has helped me out in many a scenario.

Peppers, much like potatoes and chocolate, are native to the Americas and have made impactful culenary changes to the world. It wasn't until after the expansion of europeans into the Americas that the pepper was introduced to the cuisine of Asia and Africa. Today we enjoy some form of pepper in most of the meals we enjoy, especailly the ones with heat (Specifically capsasin heat).

The versitility of Green Chili leads to confusion as it can be an uncooked product, a topping, or a meal iteself.

Peppers are found in a large amount of central american food each of which many of which are extrely delicious. Personally one of my favorite is Chili Verde con Cerdo of commonly known in Colorado and the southwest as Green Chili. This dish is as wonderful as it is versitle. It can be used as a standalone bowl full of chili for a cold winter evening or it can spice up another dish with some small variations to ingrdients and consistency.

The versitility of Green Chili leads to confusion as it can be an uncooked product, a topping, or a meal iteself. This is mostly possible because it is a fairly basic meal as far as ingredients go, and can pair well with many other meals. You take a good helping of green produce, mix in some water (or chicken stock) with pork and cook it until the consistency is soft and the pork can be easily pulled apart. It's not completely unreasonible to have it as part of breakfast, lunch and then dinner.

Ingredients

  • 1 lb Pork Shoulder or Butt
  • 1 tbsp Olive Oil
  • 6 Garlic cloves (2 minced; 4 whole)
  • 1 tbsp Cumin
  • 1 tsp Salt
  • 4-6 Green Peppers (Use the Mix n' Match Chart Below)
  • 4 Tomatillos
  • 1 Sweet/Yellow Onion (Rough Chop)
  • 2 Cups Broth or Water
  • ¼ c Cilantro (Diced)
  • 1 tbsp Cinnamon
  • 1 tbsp Honey
  • 1 tbsp Cotia Cheese (Optional but Highly Recommended)

Equipment

  • Large Heavy Bottomed Pot
  • Wooden Spoon
  • Baking Sheet
  • Broiler or Grill
  • Sealing Plastic Bag

Serving size

4 Servings

Cooking time

45 Minutes active time; 4 Hours total

Pick ya' Peppers

Heat is definilty a preference, not a requirement. Take a look at the table below and mix and match peppers to match your chili to your palete.

Pepper Heat Season
Anaheim 		500–2,500 Summer to Early Winter
Serrano 		6,000–23,000 Summer
Hatch 		2,500 Later Summer
Jalipeno 		5,000 Early Spring to Late Summer
Poblano 		1,000–1,500 Summer to Fall

Have your ingredients ready and your peppers picked out? Good! This is fairly laidback recipie but you want to be ready and don't over roast your peppers!

  1. Marinate Pork

    Cut your port shoulder into medium-large cubes (something around 1.5"-2"). Try to keep the size consistent to avoid overcooking some of the meat.

    Mix the Oil, Salt, Cumin into a large ziplock bag and mix with your hands. Once the bag is well mixed add in the pork and mix again to fully cover each of the cubes.

    Store in a refregerator for at least an hour to marinate. You can leave this in your fridge but try to use it in the next few days to avoid spoiling.

  2. Light it Up

    Start Broiler or Grill to around 500℉

  3. Prep for Roast

    Wash pepper and remove the stem and top half of the peppers and halve or quarter the rest. Remove any large bunches or unwanted.

    Removing the tip and bulb then cut the onion into 8ths.

    Remove the husk and wash your tomatillos, then cut an × into the top or bottom to make sure the main body is pierced. This is done to realease pressure and avoid explosion.

    Peel garlic cloves.

  4. Roast

    Add the peppers (skin side up), onion, tomatillos and onion to the broiler or grill and let the outsides blacken approx. 10 min.

    Once roasted, remove from heat and allow to cool.

  5. Brown Pork

    Heat a deep heavy bottomed pot to medium-hot.

    Add cubed pork to pot and cook pork to a golden brown.

  6. Chop

    Rough chop cilantro

  7. Mix-in and Cook

    Add in your broth, roasted produce, cilantro and cook on medium-high for 20 minutes. Stirring occasionally. Leave the top off of the pot so the chili thickens to your favorite viscosity.

  8. Simmer

    Turn heat to low-medium and put lid of pot to allow the chili flavors to mix without losing extra moisture.

    Check on chili every 20-30 minutes.

    You will know it is done when the pork breaks apart with little effort. Approx 2-3 Hours.

  9. Serve and Garnish

    Serve in a warm bowl and add Cotia and cilantro to top
